OPERATIONAL DEFINITIONS

 

Standard Operating Procedure (SOP): is a written procedure/instruction that helps workers to achieve quality and uniformity of routine operations across similar organizations or institutions.

 

Patient: is a sick or injured animal presented to the veterinary clinics by their owner seeking professional help. An animal  admitted to receive medical treatment.

 

Client/Owner: a person who presented their animal to the veterinary clinics seeking veterinary services.

Janitor: a person who is employed in the veterinary premises for cleaning, warden or gatekeeping, and other minor assistance.

Veterinarian: is a professional with a Doctor of Veterinary Medicine degree (DVM) holder who protects the health and well-being of animals. They diagnose and control animal diseases and treat sick and injured animals. They also advise owners on proper care of their animals.

Para-veterinarian: is a professional with a Bachelor of Veterinary Science degree (BVSc) holder who assists a veterinarian in their routine duties, or carry out animal health procedures autonomously as part of a veterinary care system.

Veterinary clinician: is a veterinarian who is in charge of handling veterinary clinical cases and perform clinical examinations and diagnosis of diseases.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

1.      BACKGROUND AND JUSTIFICATION

 

The livestock sector supports the livelihoods of a large proportion of rural households in most African countries and it is believed to play a paramount role in rural poverty reduction strategies. Ethiopia has the largest livestock population in Africa with approximately 58 million cattle, 54 million goats and sheep, plus a collection of horses, donkeys, camels and chickens, and the Oromiya region contributes the majority. The livestock sector provides draught power for crop production, source of meat and milk, and source of cash income for farmers. Furthermore, livestock provides foreign currency to the national and considerably contributes to the national GDP. However, the profitability of the sub-sector is hindered by multifaceted constraints, of which livestock diseases are the major contributing factors. The widespread prevalence of a wide range of diseases and parasites in all agro-ecological zones of the country causes direct economic losses through high mortality of animals and contributes to the poor productive and reproductive performance of the animals. Consequences are also devastating at the national level since the presence of the diseases has triggered strict trade embargo amounting to be hundreds of millions of birr losses annually.

 

The HEARD (Health of Ethiopian Animals for Rural Development) is an EU funded program aimed to increase and sustain livestock productivity and improve livestock product marketing in Ethiopia through better veterinary service delivery. The program encompasses three strategic components: strengthening the quality of public and private veterinary services, improving the competence (knowledge, skill and attitude) of veterinary professionals at the different levels and better control of zoonotic diseases through ensuring the safety of animal products along the livestock value chain. The program partners with several stakeholders to achieve these goals among which are the national universities such as Addis Ababa University, College of Veterinary Medicine and Agriculture and the Oromiya Livestock Resource Development Agency (OLRDA).

 

Generally, the role of SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) to achieve uniformity and quality in routine operations is indispensable. Accordingly, this clinical examination and diagnosis SOPs aimed to set operational instruction for the animal health professionals across the Oromiya region. It is collaboratively prepared by the Oromiya Livestock Resource Development Agency (OLRDA) and Addis Ababa University, College of Veterinary Medicine and Agriculture (AAU-CVMA).

2.      OBJECTIVES

 

General objective:

  • To standardize the veterinary clinical examinations and diagnosis approaches in veterinary clinics across the Oromiya region, thereby optimizing veterinary service delivery and quality.

 

Specific objectives:

  • To ensure standardized procedures of veterinary examinations and diagnosis in all veterinary clinics across the Oromiya region and at all times.
  • To assure safe and humane handling of animals during clinical examination procedures.
  • To clarify the roles and responsibilities of veterinarians or animal health personnel providing veterinary service.
  • To ease communications between professionals.
  • To improve the veterinary clinical examination standards continually.

4.      RESTRAINING SOPs

 

If not restrained, animals could be dangerous to the clinicians, technical assistants, owners, and equipment in the clinic premises. Therefore, safe and humane restraint of animals is of great importance during clinical examination and treatment.

 

4.1. Considerations and pre-requisites

 

  • Veterinary clinical premises need to be free of noise
  • Basic knowledge of animal behavior according to their species is important.
  • Environmental hazard to the restrained animal should be considered
  • Measure animals’ reactions upon your approach and proceed accordingly.
  • If possible, take vital parameters earlier than restraining or apply lesser pressure/force to minimize changes to vital parameters if restraining is mandatory before taking vital signs.
  • Call the name of the animal (if known) during examination and treatment to make them feel comfortable and get familiar easily (verbal restraint).
  • Chemical restraint needs to be done by a surgeon/veterinarian only.

 

4.2. Purpose

 

This SOP is intended to provide a guideline on how to conduct a restraint during an examination, treatment, vaccination, and general handling of animals without compromising animals’ welfare.

 

4.3. Responsible personnel

 

Owner, janitor, animal health technician, other clients can be invited in.

 

4.4.Materials

 

  • Bull holder
  • Ropes
  • Crush
  • Nose twitch
  • Stock
  • Halter
  • Muzzle
  • Towel
  • Neck belt
  • Gauze

 

4.5. Procedures

 

4.5.1.      Cattle restraint

 

Use one of the following methods or combination of them to restrain cattle:

Crush: If available, walk cattle into a crush before any procedure. A crush can preferably be constructed from metal posts but woods can also be used. Use a rope around the base of the horns and secure to a pole/crush

Nasal lead: Grip the nasal septum between your thumb and index finger or using a bull holder.

Leg raising: Elevating the front right/left leg with a rope and passing the rope over the neck to pull it at the front.

Casting (rope-squeeze):

  • If possible, fast the animal before twelve hours of casting.
  • Get enough manpower and strong rope (not very slim/thin)
  • Select the site of casting carefully preferably with plenty of grass and level ground. Avoid bricks and stones.
  • Make two ties at both ends of the belly using a single rope. Pull both ends of the rope with two people at the front and back of the animal. The person at the backside of the animal pulls the rope to the right to let the animal fall on the right side.
  • Cast on the right not to compromise the rumen motility.
  • Do not leave the animal cast over a long duration of time as that might cause bloat.
  • As much as possible, refrain from casting pregnant animals.
  • In calves: stand on one of the sides (depending on your convenience) and bend over the animal. Reach down and grasp the nearest fore- and hind-leg. Lift the animal on your thigh and slide gently to the ground.
  • Have other people around you to secure the extremities or tie using a rope.
  • Beware of sharp toes of calves.

 

4.5.2.      Equine restraint

 

Considerations (peculiarities)

  • Equines have to be approached only from the front and the left side around the shoulder area.
  • Horses can bite, kick caudally, and paw using the forelimbs.
  • During casting, care should be taken for the head of equine

Use one of the following methods or combination of them to restrain equines/working animals:

Stock/crush: provides bodily confinement and the back door of the stock has to be padded to prevent injury during a back kick.

Haltering: is a basic restraint for working animals. Apply from the left side of the animal around the head.

Hand/mechanical twitching: The loose skin cranial to the shoulder could be pinched to divert attention. A short wooden handle with a chain loop or short rope could also be used to twitch the upper lip.

Limb lifting: This stops the animal from kicking. One of the forelimbs could be lifted to allow examination from the side of the lifted limb.

Casting (double side-line) in horses:

  • Pass a long double around the neck and fasten in a collar form.
  • Apply a knot on the neck in a figure 8 fashion
  • Carry the free end between the fore-limbs.
  • Pass between the hind-limbs and take around the pastern joints of the hind-limbs.
  • Bring the ends again to the front and fasten to the collar. Refer Fig. below
  • In foals: Foals are wild and need to be handled carefully. Several people are needed to hold foals down without harm. Small foals could be held in front of the chest and behind the rump to immobilize. Beware of mares as they get aggressive when they think their foal is in danger.

4.5.3.      Small ruminant restraint

 

Considerations

  • Generally, small ruminants are less harmful compared to other animals.
  • Sheep are herd-oriented animals which necessitate retraining when they are together.

Use one of the following methods or combination of them to restrain small ruminants:

Rope collars: are usually important to get a point to grasp to immobilize the animal.

Holding: let the owner put one hand over the chest and the other on the ramp to immobilize the animal

Straddling: this could be achieved by holding and squeezing the animal between legs at the shoulder point.

Casting: Refer ‘Casting in calves’

4.5.4.    Small animal restraint

 

Considerations

Generally, small animals are more harmful (in terms of biting, transmission of rabies) as compared to other animals. 

Cat restraint

Neck scruff: Hold the loose skin at the back of the neck slightly towards the head. This stops the cat from biting.

Towel restraint: The entire cat could be covered in a towel and the part needed for examination or treatment can be exposed.

Dog restraint

Gauze muzzle: Make a tie with gauze underneath the mouth part and take the ends to the back of the neck (slightly towards the head) and make another tie.

Muzzle: Commercial muzzles made up of leather or fabrics can be purchased.

Towel restraint: can be used for puppies. Refer ‘Towel restraint’ in cats

Neck belt: can be used by the owners to temporarily hold the animal until another restraint method is applied.

 

4.5.5.    Camel restraint

 

Considerations

  • Camel is very strong and capable of biting and kicking.
  • Camel should mainly be restrained by the owner or trained technician.
  • Always make sure that the camel’s head is restrained.

 

Limb raising: Raise one of the forelimbs and flex at the elbow to tie it to the arm.

                         

Crouching: Let the camel assume sternal recumbency. Tie both forelimbs to the neck and the hind limbs to the rump.

                         

 

5.      VETERINARY CLINICAL EXAMINATION SOPs

 

Clinical examination is a vital part of the process of veterinary diagnosis. It helps to acquire information required to know the disease affecting the health of the animal or disorders producing the clinical abnormalities. Besides, it provides organs or systems involved, the location, type of lesion present, the pathophysiological processes occurring, and the severity of the disease. A clinical examination comprises three components: the history, the examination, and the reasoning, where the veterinarian discusses the nature and consequences of the clinical findings. Clients seek veterinarian help for three main reasons: diagnostic purposes (help to sell or buy animal), treatment or reassurance, or a combination of these. Thus, investigation of any animal disease must be undertaken carefully with thorough clinical examinations to recognize the nature of the disease, provide effective treatment, and recommend practicable prevention and control measures.

 

5.1.  Considerations

 

  • Basic knowledge of anatomy, physiology, pathology, animal behavior, and disease epidemiology is important.
  • Veterinary clinical examination skills, knowledge of etiology, clinical signs, and pathogenesis of the diseases are the basic requirements for clinicians to make a diagnosis.
  • It is necessary to use suitable means of restraint to complete examination safely and without harm to the clinician or his assistants and the animal under examination.
  • Clinical examination of an animal must be accompanied by a consideration of its surroundings and environments.

 

5.2.  Purpose

 

This SOP is designed to standardize clinical examinations and diagnosis in domestic animals that help veterinarians and para-veterinarians to prescribe opportune treatment (to individual or group of animals), give an accurate prognosis, and recommend applicable actions in animal disease control and preventions. 

 

5.3.  Responsible personnel

 

Veterinarians (DVM), para-veterinarians (BVS), and animal health assistants trained to perform clinical examinations and diagnosis of animal diseases.

 

5.4.  Materials

 

  • PPE (Personal Protective Equipment): White Gown, Gloves, overall, boats, eye goggle, face mask.
  • Diagnosis aids: Thermometer (digital or mercury), stethoscope, stopwatch, hammer-pleximeter, nasogastric tube, stomach tube, trocar and cannula, catheter, mouth gag, vaginal speculum, plastic rectal gloves, lubricant, hoof testers and clippers, Californian milk test reagent.
  • Record keeping materials: Case book, prescription paper, out-patient follow-up card.
  • Cleaning and disinfection materials: Antiseptics, detergents, disinfectants.

5.5.  Procedures

 

5.5.1.     Clinician-client communication approaches

 

Reliable and well organized medical history can only be obtained with good communication skills of the clinician when approaching the client. All questions which are directed to the client will ultimately assist the diagnosis process and help identify the cause of the disease accurately.

 

Points to be considered during the clinician-client approach:

 

  • A clinician needs to be in a white professional dressing so that the clients know who to talk to regarding their animals.
  • Speaking the regional language and knowing the societal norm is helpful to better communicate and understand the client.
  • Have time to greet the owner and if possible ask how they are doing on that particular day.
  • Ask all questions with a purpose in mind and make a good memory of them not to ask the client over and over again.
  • Use as minimum technical terms as possible and avoid technical jargon, ambiguous, and leading questions.
  • Use open-ended questions and then allow your clients to answer your questions and get ready to listen carefully.
  • Clients usually know their animals in detail and reported subtle changes in behavior should not be undermined.
  • Opinions expressed regarding the etiology by the client should be viewed with caution as they can be misleading.

5.5.2.      History taking

 

History is the backbone of the whole process of clinical examination. It is presented to the clinician through the medium of the owner’s complaint, which is a request for professional assistance. For the completeness and accuracy of history taking, the following points should be well documented;

 

Patient data/ information:

  • Owner’s information: owner’s name, address, kebele, telephone, peasant association, etc.
  • Patient signalment: Species, breed, sex, age, name, ID No., body weight, color, markings, horned or polled, and other identification marks of the patient.

Present history:

  • Ask for the duration of the disease: When the disease has started? (Whether it is per-acute, acute, sub-acute or chronic).
  • Ask for clinical signs/symptoms observed: (appetite for food or drink, defecation (manner (straining or free-fall), consistency, color, and frequency), urination (manner (dripping/normal flow), color, frequency, and transparency), respiration, sweating, physical activity, milk production, growth, gait, posture, voice, odor, etc.).
  • Obtain information on the number of animals affected: (morbidity rate and mortality rate).
  • Ask for treatment given (if any): traditionally treated or treatment that has been given before coming to the clinic.

Note: When a group/flock/ of animal is affected, select a sample or one animal for history taking.

 

Past history

  • Focus on the previous history of disease/s which had affected the individual animal or the heard (if any).
  • If there were previous diseases, gather details of clinical signs seen, diagnosis performed, treatments given, the number of animals affected, and died.
  • Get information on any new arrival to the established flock/heard (focus on the health history and status of the source animals).
  • Check for the system of animal replacement used on the farm or in the home.

 

Management and environmental history

  • Gather information on animal management (feeding, housing, hygiene, breeding, and production performance) and their environment/geography (topography, climate, water bodies, rainfall, and soil type).

5.5.3.     Physical Examinations

 

Inspection: is a hands-off approach and could best be undertaken during the period devoted to obtaining the case history and before the animals get disturbed (restrained).

Focus on the following general appearance of the animal:

  • Behaviour: check the way the animal responds to the external stimuli (note the level of brightness or dullness) and also observe the animal while they move, stand, and laying down and recumbent.
  • Posture: observe the animal for any arching of the back, abduction of the elbows, the elevation of the tail, and the way they are recumbent.
  • Gait: note their gait for any lameness, stiff gait, and walking in circles.
  • Body condition: observe and grade their body condition as normal, thin, emaciated, fat, or obesity.
  • Conformation: check for any derangement in symmetry, shape, and size of different body regions.
  • Skin and coat: hair or wool changing (how rough or lustier), alopecia, hyperkeratosis, scabs, and any sweating should be checked.
  • Eating: check for any abnormalities of prehension, mastication, and swallowing.
  • Defecation and urination: observe the manner, colour, content, and frequency of defecation and urination.
  • Voice: check the voice for hoarse, weak, bellowing, and yawing.
  • Respiration: look for type, depth, rate, rhythm, and any noises during respiration.
  • Abdomen: see the symmetry of the abdomen (changed if there is bloat) and ruminal contraction.
  • External genitalia, orifices, and mammary glands of female animals should also be checked before approaching the animals.

Palpation

  • Restrain the animal properly before proceeding to this procedure (refer to restraining SOPs).
  • Use fingers, palm, back of the hand, and fist, to get the information about changes in size, shape, consistency, and temperature and presence of pain in the body or tissues.

Percussion

  • Strike a part of the body using finger-finger percussion or hammer-pleximeter method as indicated in figure 4 below and evaluate the condition of the organ or body part being percussed based on the echoed audible sounds.
  • Apply on the thorax (for lungs and heart), paranasal sinuses, and subcutaneous emphysema.

            

 

Auscultation

  • Use a stethoscope to increase the acuity and stopwatch for counting (mainly for respiratory and heart rate)
  • Always check your stethoscope is properly functioning before approaching the animal.
  • Pure sound is only heard when the animal and the environment is quiet.
  • Fix the probe of the stethoscope firmly on the area of the organ being auscultated.
  • Apply to evaluate the state of the respiratory tract, heart and alimentary tract (rumen motility, gut sound)

 

5.5.4.      Vital signs (TRP)

 

Temperature

  • Always ensure that the animal is restrained properly.
  • DO NOT approach the animals from their ‘blind area’.
  • Sterilize the thermometer with disinfectants.
  • Shake the thermometer (only for a mercury thermometer).
  • Lubricate the bulb end of the thermometer.
  • Evacuate the rectum.
  • Insert the thermometer into a rectum rotationally and gently.
  • Check the bulb of the thermometer remains in contact with the rectal mucous membrane.
  • Keep the thermometer in site for at least 3-5 minutes (for mercury thermometer) or wait until you hear ‘beep’ sound for a digital thermometer.
  • Pull out the thermometer, read the number, and compare it with the normal parameters in table 2 below.
  • Explain how hot or cold the body is using the term fever, hypothermia, and afebrile or normal.
  • Wipe the thermometer with disinfectant before storage or use on the next patient.

 

Temperature conversions: °C = (°F – 32) × 5/9

                                               °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

Pulse

  • Always be sure that the animal is restrained (see restraining SOPs).
  • Locate the artery used for measuring the pulse (varied in different species of animals).
  • Place the ball part of one or more fingers on the skin over the selected artery.
  • DO NOT use your thumb (it has its pulse that you may feel).
  • Note the pressure or pulsation of the arteries felt on your finger digits.
  • Assess the rate by counting the number of beats per minute or count at least for 30seconds and multiply by 2(two) and compare it with normal parameters indicated in Table 2 below.
  • Also, note the rhythm and quality of the pulsation.

 

Respiration

Use one or the combination of the following methods:

 

Auscultation: refer auscultation procedures above

  • Is the most preferred method.
  • Use a stethoscope and stopwatch.
  • Locate the lung area (on both sides).
  • Assess the respiratory rate by counting the number of breaths per minute or count at least for 30seconds and multiply by 2(two) and relate with the normal parameters in Table 2 below.
  • Also, listen and assess the sounds, quality (strength), and rhythm of the breaths for any abnormality.

Inspection:

  • Stand behind and to one side of the animal,
  • Notice a height and drop of the abdominal muscle, the chest, and flank where each height and drop is considered as one breathing.
  • Check for any difficulty of breathing.
  • Listen to the sounds (commonly heard when there is inspiratory or expiratory dyspnea).

Palpation:

  • Put one hand in front of the nostril to feel the exchange of the gas and count (effective in a cold environment) or
  • Put one hand on the lung area or the thorax to feel the respiratory movements.

5.5.5.     Examination of the state of peripheral circulation

 

Examination of the visible mucus membrane

Inspect the visible mucous membranes such as the conjunctiva (most commonly used), nasal, oral, and vaginal mucus membranes to assess the state of peripheral circulation. 

Conjunctival examination

  • Always ensure that you are on the safest side of the animal.
  • Evert one of the eyelids (upper eyelid, lower eyelid or nictitating membrane (3rd eyelid)) and check for their color,
  • Interpretation of the color changes:
    • Pink or rose-red in horses, light red in cattle: normal
    • Pale (whitish): abnormal and indicate the existence of anemia or shock.
    • Congested (reddened): the presence of fever and infectious disease.
    • Icteric (yellowish): jaundice.
    • Cyanotic (bluish): the existence of heart or lung dysfunction.
    • Petechial hemorrhages: the existence of infectious diseases and dysfunction of the cardiovascular system

Capillary Refill Time (CRT)

  • Take care of aggressive and biting animals.
  • Compress the gingiva or oral mucosa or vulva with a finger until it turns to pale (whitish color),
  • Leave the pale area,
  • Record how long it takes to return to its normal state (pink color),
  • Interpretation:
    • Normal: CRT return in less than 2 seconds
    • Indicate a developing problem: CRT takes between 2 and 5 seconds
    • Abnormal: CRT return takes more than 5 seconds

 

 

5.5.6.      Systemic clinical examinations

 

Considerations:

 

  • Always ensure that the animal is restrained properly.
  • DO NOT approach animals from their ‘blind area’.
  • Have a record of any abnormality appreciated during this procedure.
  • Broadly diagnose the organ or system suspected of being affected.

 

Examination of the head and neck regions

  • Take care while you approach horned animals.
  • Inspect the head of the animal for symmetry.
  • Observe the general head position (tilting to one side) and test for normal cranial nerve responses.
  • Check the nares for patency and any discharge and classify the discharge as serous, purulent, hemorrhagic, mucoid or mucopurulent and as unilateral or bilateral.
  • Examine the muzzle (moistened or dried) and observe lips for lesions,
  • Assess mouth or oral mucous membranes, teeth, hard and soft palate, tongue, pharynx, tonsils,
  • Assess the eyes for size, position, symmetry, and vision.
  • Check eyes for discharge from conjunctiva, sclera, pupil, cornea, lens inflammation, redness, uneven/abnormal pupil size, corneal clouding, squinting.
  • Evaluate carriage and position of ears (droopy ear (bilateral or unilateral) and rigidity of ear), thickness/malleability of pinnae (due to edema or swelling), and cleanliness of ear canals (pus accumulation and foreign bodies).
  • Inspect and palpate the jaws for any swelling or pain.
  • Palpate the lymph nodes of the head.

Examination of the skin and hair coat

  • Look at the hair coat (shiny or rough).
  • Examine the skin for lesions such as alopecia or hypotrichosis, itching or scratching, inflammations or allergic reactions, ectoparasites, the color of the skin, and hair (depigmentation, cyanosis), trauma, and swelling.
  • Assess the hydration level of the animal by performing a skin tent (prolonged skin tent indicates dehydration).

Examinations of the thoracic regions (lung area)

  • Inspect the lung area and assess the respiratory movements or types.
  • Also, feel the nasal air movements on your palm or the back of the hand (you can assess the rate using this method).
  • Assess the upper respiratory tract by palpating the trachea (if a cough can be induced)
  • Auscultate the trachea (to check for airflow or bronchial sounds).
  • Auscultate the lower respiratory tract (the lungs) to assess lung sounds (alveolar sounds), rate, rhythm, and quality or depth of the respiration.
  • Also, percuss the lung area and consider the sounds (as resonant or dully).
  • Evaluate both sides similarly.

 

Examination of the heart

  • Auscultate the heart (on the left side) and assess the animal’s heart rate, sound, strength, and rhythm.
  • Observe the jugular vein and mammary vein for any signs of distension or pulsation.

 

Examination of the digestive system

  • Start with assessing the appetite
  • Examine the oral cavity (oral mucous membranes for any lesions, drooling (profuse salivation), regurgitation of ingesta, protrusion of the tongue, smacking of the lips, abnormal prehension, difficulty or painful mastication),
  • Palpate pharynx and esophagus area for any swelling, chock (foreign body) and other abnormalities,

Examine the abdomen

In ruminants:

  • Observe for any bulging out of the rumen or abdomen.
  • Assess the rumen contractions by auscultating the rumen in the left para-lumbar fossa with the stethoscope or use your fist and gently push into the left para-lumbar fossa at least for one minute.

Note: normal rumen contraction is 1-3 per one minute or 3-5 per five minutes

  • Check for TRP (Traumatic Reticulo Peritonitis) using wither pinch, raising the ventral aspect of the trunk (stick method used sometimes) or moving the animal downhill.

In horses:

  • Auscultate the abdomen on the four quadrants (upper left (stomach), lower left (colon), upper right (cecum), and lower right (colon)) and assess the intestinal sounds.
  • Check for colic syndrome or other abdominal discomforts.

In small animals:

  • Palpate abdomen using 1 or 2 hands, begin at the spine and move ventrally, allowing the abdominal viscera to slip through the fingers.
  • Repeat throughout the abdomen, noting organ size and location and the presence of, fluid, gas, fetuses, masses, or feces.
  • Note any pain or resisting during palpation.

 

Rectal examination

  • Always check the animal is restrained properly.
  • Wear rectal or arm length glove and lubricate.
  • Cone shape the fingers and insert the hand in a rotating way.
  • Use tranquilizers in horses.
  • In the bloat case, the pressures developed in the rumen push back your hand and make the procedure difficult.

Examination of the urogenital system

  • Observe urination by inspecting the posture, urination frequency, urine color, and concentration.
  • Palpate the kidney to determine size, consistency, and presence of pain (at the ventral aspect of the transverse process of the 3rd to 5th lumbar vertebrae).
  • Assess urinary bladder by inducing urination (massage the lower part between the two hind legs in small animals or rectal palpation in large animals). See the rectal palpation procedure above.
  • Inspect and palpate the testes, prepuce and the penis after withdrawing from the prepuce,
  • Observe external female genitalia for lesions, discharge, and other abnormalities,
  • Use a vaginal speculum to examine the inside part of the vagina and intrarectal palpation for the cervix, uterus, and ovaries.

 

Examination of udder and teat

  • Be sure that the hind legs are properly restrained.
  • Inspect and palpate the teats (for lesions or discharges).
  • Check udder/mammary gland (for change in size, color, swelling of supra mammary lymph nodes, temperature, lesions, and pain).
  • In lactating or suckling animals draw the first few foremilks and notice the appearance (watery, clotted, contain blood, and pus).
  • Use the California Mastitis Test (CMT) reagent to assess for subclinical mastitis. (refer laboratory SOP).

 

Examinations of the nervous and musculoskeletal system

  • Appreciate the behavior of the animal (level of brightness or dullness).
  • See the way they respond to stimuli by creating a sound (active, dull, excited).
  • Examine the brain by corneal reflex, the pupil reflex.
  • Examine the spinal cord of the neck and thorax by withers reflex.
  • Examine the spinal cord of the back part by applying hoof stimuli and anus stimuli.
  • Look at the way the animal walk, lay down, and stand to judge musculoskeletal pain and lameness.
  • Inspect and palpate the joints of long bones and different muscles (gluteal, neck muscles).

 

Examination of superficial lymph nodes of animals

  • Inspect and palpate the superficial lymph nodes.
  • Appreciate their size, pain reaction, tenderness, lobulation, consistency, abscess formation, maturation, and discharge.
  • Assess the temperature of the skin overlying the lymph nodes, adhesion to the surrounding tissue, and whether the involvement is bilateral or unilateral.

 

  • Decision making

 

Tentative diagnosis

  • Analyze the history and clinical findings acquired during the clinical examinations.
  • Also, consider the pathophysiology and epidemiology of diseases.
  • List the possible diseases (Differential Diagnosis) that best describe the condition.
  • In case of further investigation (Confirmatory Diagnosis) is mandatory, collect samples before treatment is instituted.

 

Confirmatory or definitive diagnosis

  • Fill the laboratory request form for required sample collection and laboratory tests. See laboratory SOP.
  • Receive laboratory results from responsible personnel, read, and make a decision.

 

5.5.7.    Treatment and prognosis

 

  • Schedule treatment and prescribe drugs based on the conclusions made (tentatively or definitively). Refer treatment SOPs
  • Draw your prognosis by considering the extent of the disease and the expected response to therapy.
  • Follow-up the patient either in the clinic during the successive therapies or at home for the diseases with prolonged recovery.

 

5.5.9.     Owner advice

  • Approach the client generously.
  • Tell the client about the disease in a comprehensible way.
  • DO NOT forget to aware of the client to follow the treatment regimen.
  • Be smart while advising and focus on practicable means of prevention.
  • Update your clients to visit veterinary clinics or animal health posts immediately after any disease signs are appreciated.
  • Give them something to read (if any) on available vaccine types, vaccination schedules of the clinic, and prevention measures of livestock diseases having economic and public health significance.

6.      MEDICAL RECORD KEEPING

 

The medical record is a document containing sufficient data written in sequences of events to justify the diagnosis, the treatment, and other procedures done in the veterinary premises. The veterinary clinic shall maintain an adequate medical record for every patient admitted as an outpatient, inpatient, or emergency patient, which shall be documented accurately with all significant clinical and other information promptly. The medical records shall be assembled as a format or sheet to easily understand and save time.

 

Importance of medical records in a veterinary clinic:

  • Support further follow-up and treatment of patients.
  • Safeguard integrity and communication among staff members.
  • For research and training purposes.
  • A tool for disease surveillance and controlling epidemic diseases.
  • Helpful to manage and monitor the clinic.
