OPERATIONAL DEFINITIONS

 

A standard operating procedure (SOP):  is a set of step-by-step instructions compiled by an organization to help workers carry out complex routine operations.

Cream: a viscous semisolid, consisting of oil in water emulsion or water in oil emulsion.

Dusting powder: popular antibacterial agent applied on animal wounds.

Inhalation dosage forms: gaseous and volatile liquid anaesthetic agent (drugs), given by inhalation, e.g., Halothane

Intramuscular (IM) route: is the injection of a substance directly into the muscle.

Intravenous route (IV): refer to the administration into the venous circulation of a solution containing antibiotics.

Lotion: an aqueous solution or suspension for local application.

Ointment: semisolid preparation for external application.

Oral dosage forms: refers to solid oral dosage forms

Oral route of administration: is a route of administration where a substance is taken through the mouth.

Parenteral dosage forms: all products administered other than oral route

Parenteral route of administration: is refers to a drug administration by injection directly in to the tissue fluid or blood without having to cross the intestinal mucosa.

Spray-a drug applied in liquid form by pressure.

Sub conjunctiva: refer to the administration of substance directly into the conjunctiva.

Subcutaneous (SC) route: Is the injection of a substance directly into the skin.

Vaccination: introduction of a vaccine in to the body to produce immunity to a specific disease.

Veterinary vaccines: preparations containing antigenic substances administered for the purpose of inducing a specific and active immunity against disease provoked.

1.      Introduction

Oromia has by far the largest number of livestock resource in Ethiopia. This indicates that Oromia’s high potential contribution of livestock sub-sector to both the regional and national economic development. In spites of huge numbers of livestock production and productivity has remained very low. Among many other animal production and productivity limiting constraints, animal disease is one of the major causes of low livestock production and productivity.

 

Among 15 list A and 72 list B diseases identified by the World Animal Health Organization (OIE). 7 and 20 respectively are confirmed to exist in Ethiopia and Oromia too. Most of these diseases are preventable by vaccination.

 

For vaccination to be effective, the specific groups of animals affected, the period at which outbreak occurs and their distribution in a certain geographic area have to be conserved.

 According to DACA (2007, p-18) Animal health service is at risk concerning drug usage, that calls for immediate action to pull out the service from the chronic but grave situation of drug use. The available drugs must be presented, dispensed and used rationally. Irrational use of drugs can lead to drug resistance, wastage of drugs, unnecessary expenditure on drugs and lack of confidence on the service and professionals involved.

 

Misuse of antimicrobial agents reducing effectiveness of treatment, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) jeopardizes the control of animal and human infectious diseases. Only well-trained veterinarians should administer antimicrobial agents to animals. In order to improve veterinary service quality, standardization of the service is the base to drive all veterinary service activities to ward prevention, control and eradication of livestock diseases.

 

Therefore, veterinary service standardization, drugs and vaccines supply and usage are the main activities that need basic change to be improved. The development of SOPs for veterinary vaccination and treatment help standardisation of veterinary services i.e. quality output and uniformity of the service so that strengthen the public and private partnerships.

4.      SOPs FOR VETERINARY VACCINATION

 

These Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) consider different routes of vaccine administration, vaccination procedure, injection sites of different species, responsibility, equipment and consumable material required to enhance quality of the services provided.

4.1.Vaccine shipment and transportation

 

 Consideration

  • Perform of hand hygienebefore administering vaccines by any route.
  • Properly document the receipt/issue of vaccine from store.
  • Adequate cold chain maintenance during transportation should be considered.
  • Appropriate packing materials (Cool box, thermo-cool box with ice pack) for safe shipment / temporarily storage of vaccine.
  • Properly label receiver/sender details on top of the container
  • Avoid leaving containers in areas where they are exposed to direct sunlight.
  • Avoid transporting vaccines with other materials/commodities.

 

 Purpose:

  • To move or transport veterinary vaccines from area of production or distribution to the area of its implementation/ distribution using appropriate packing materials (cold chain system) for maximum protection.

 

Responsibility

 

Veterinary drugs and Vaccines are transported only by veterinary professionals and paraprofessionals (veterinary doctors, Bachelor of veterinary science (BVSc) and animal health assistances)

 

Materials and Equipment’s

  • Care/vehicle with fixed appropriate cold chain system, Cool box, thermo- cool box with ice pack.
  • An insulated cooler with cold packs is ideal for vaccine transport
  • Sufficient materials needed for vaccine transport

Procedures

  • Prepare vaccine issues documentation from store,
  • Prepare appropriate packing materials that provide the maximum protection. Because, vaccines must only be transported using appropriate packing materials.
  • The container should be labelled clearly.
  • Move transport containers to a vehicle that is already at a suitable temperature, neither too hot nor too cold.
  • The total time for transport should be a maximum of 8 hours.
  • Check vaccine temperature upon arrival at vaccine storage facility, open and inspects it immediately for any obvious abnormalities, such as discoloration or particulate formation and store at recommended temperatures immediately.
  • Mobile practices should be including a thermometer to monitor temperature during transportation.
  • Place the cooler in the vehicle and not outside the passenger compartment (e.g., trunk or truck bed).
  • When transporting vaccines, place them in a cooler with ice packs.

          Proper clearance document should be given at handing over of vaccines.  

4.2.Vaccine storage and handling

  Consideration

  •  Never store the vaccine in the ice chamber unless indicated by the manufacturer.
  • Never leave vaccine out of the refrigerator or freezer when not in use
  • Ensure that the refrigerator is under annual maintenance contract for speedy repairs.
  • Live antigens may be inactivated by disinfectants or alcohol therefore these substances mustn’t be used to sterilize syringes.
  • Ensure adequate space for air circulation.
  • Open the refrigerator only when absolutely necessary.
  • Do not store food, drinks or specimens etc. alongside vaccines.
  • Discard expired vaccine products in appropriate medical waste containers
  • Do not interchange diluents or dilute vaccine with other compounds such as sterile water.

 

Purpose

The main purpose of proper storage and handling of Vaccines is to keep their effectiveness by providing the maximum protection.

 

Responsibility:

Store keeper in storage, handling and other professionals on duty and at field activity

Materials and Equipment

  • Properly designed and constructed store with air circulation/ ventilation
  • Overall , guan
  • Deep fridge, Refrigerator with thermometer.
  • Shelf, ice box, ice pack
  • Fire extinguisher, wall thermometer and instrument for humidity measurement.

 

 Procedures

  • Keep the refrigerator locked at all times. The keys must be stored securely.
  • Protect vaccine vial from exposure to direct sun light
  • Store under the conditions recommended by the manufacturer. (See annex. I.)
  • Refrigerated store at 2˚C to 8˚C is usually necessary.
  • Unless otherwise specified, some vaccines such as Marek’s disease vaccine should be stored in solid carbon dioxide or liquid nitrogen.

4.3.Vaccine administration

Consideration

  • Species specific methods of handling and restraint for vaccination
  • When administering any vaccine it is important to use proper techniques, route of administration, and appropriate injection site according to the manufacturer’s recommendation.
  • When administering a vaccine by injection, correct needle size based on the route, age, patient size, and injection technique should be considered.
  • To be used, vaccine should be approved by the authorised body (VDFACA)..
  • Environmental and nutritional stress factors should be considered for maximum protection.
  • Strategies for the use and measurement of the success of vaccination in the control of animal disease have to be considered in the context of epidemiology of that specific disease.
  • Some live vaccines may be able to cross the placenta and cause abortion or fetal abnormalities.
  • Risk associated with handling and vaccinating animals in late pregnancy should be considered. .
  • In some vaccines booster vaccination needs to be given at intervals depending on whether the vaccine is inactivated or living.
  •   For most killed vaccines yearly revaccination is recommended,
  •   For some diseases which have seasonal incidence vaccination should be planned for a time        prior to the expected disease outbreak.
  •   All vaccination schemes, whether they are for an individual animal or for a herd health     vaccination program must be planned considering the presence and extent of maternal             antibody.
  •   Vaccination is primarily given pre-exposure; however, post exposure vaccine is also   administered for diseases with very long incubation period e.g. Rabies.
  •   A new needle should be used for injecting each animal when there is concern about spreading   diseases between animals;
  •   Ensure all needles are accounted for before and after administration;
  •   Only sterile needles and syringes should be used for vaccination and injections given with       aseptic precautions to avoid the possibility of abscess formation or the transmission of       incidental infections.
  •   If multiple vaccinations is giving to one animal, do not give them in the same site;   
  •   Do not administer more than 10ML in any one site, and separate injection sites by at least   four inches;
  •   The number of vaccines administered at a time i.e. within one month should not exceed six.   They are desirable from both convenience aspect and the cost-saving of reduced handling and   vaccination time. (STG for vet. Practice, p.448)
  •    Administer vaccines within 60 minutes of reconstitution.
  •   Do not reconstitute vaccines in the morning for administration throughout the day.
  •   Protect reconstituted vaccines from temperature extremes until administration

 

Purpose

  • To control animal infections and infestations, thus improving animal health and animal welfare;
  • To control anthropozoonoses and food poisoning, thereby protecting public health;
  • To solve problems associated with antibiotic and anthelmintic resistance.

 

Responsibility

Veterinary Vaccines are administered only by veterinary professionals and paraprofessionals, (veterinary doctors, bachelor of veterinary science (BVS) and animal health assistances that have practiced on performing on any species of animals).

Material and equipment

  • Protective cloth, glove and manual
  • Disinfectants (alcohol, savlon and/or Iodine)
  • Diluents:-saline water, distilled water and tape water (chemical free).
  • Vaccines (dead and live vaccine)
  • Automatic and manual vaccination syringe (different size),
  • Vaccination needles (different size),dropper needle holder, graduate cylinder,
  • Crash and ropes (restraining),
  • Ice boxes and Ice pack, deep freezers /refrigerator ,collar bags,
  • Gauze and cotton rolls, Thermometer
  • Electrical and non-electrical sterilizers,
  • Identifying material (marker), buttagas, different cylinders, jags,
  • Recording book.

4.3.2.      Vaccination procedure

 

4.3.2.1.Subcutaneous Administration

 

Subcutaneous injection is particularly convenient in small companion animals where the loose skin at the back of the neck is a commonly used route. Potential disadvantages are slower uptake of antigen as compared to a more vascular site such as muscle. Lower antibody response has been obtained with the S/C route as compared to intramuscular injection. Injection site is best around the neck or in the loose tissue behind the front leg of an animal.

  • Withdraw vaccine doses from the cool box as required.
  • Prepare vaccine when ready to administer. Preparation vaccines should be done in a designated area away from any space where potentially contaminated items are placed.
  • Read manufacturers’ instruction of the administered route and dosage
  • Gently shake bottle before withdrawing vaccine.
  • Fix a separately placed sterile needle on automatic or manual vaccination syringe. Needle used for injecting animals must not be inserted into vaccine vials.
  • Insert a separate sterile needle in to vaccine vials and withdraw vaccine.
  • Set the dose and adjust the automatic/manual vaccination syringe according to the recommended dose to the species of animals
  • Properly restrain the animal
  • Pinch a fold of skin to form a ‘<‘ in the neck skin
  • Insert needle under the raised skin into the area between the skin & muscle burying approximately ¾ the length of the needle
  • Inject the vaccine under the skin
  • Withdraw the needle in one swift motion;
  • Gently rub the injection site to relieve discomfort.

                    

            

4.3.2.2.Intramuscular injection (I/M)

Intramuscular injection is injected into muscle tissue. This route deposits vaccine into location of high vascularity and provides efficient exposure of antigen to the immune system. Attention must be paid to the anatomical choice of vaccination site to ensure adequate delivery and exposure to responsive cells.

  • Withdraw vaccine doses from the cool box as required.
  • Prepare vaccine when ready to administer. Preparation vaccines should be done in a designated area away from any space where potentially contaminated items are placed.
  • Read manufacturers’ instruction of the administered route and dosage
  • Gently shake bottle before withdrawing vaccine.
  • Fix a separately placed sterile needle on automatic or manual vaccination syringe. Needle used for injecting animals must not be inserted into vaccine vials.
  • Insert a separate sterile needle in to vaccine vials and withdraw vaccine.
  • Set the dose and adjust the automatic/manual vaccination syringe according to the recommended dose to the species of animals
  • Properly restrain the animal
  • Disinfect injection site properly
  • Push the needle through the skin ,burying approximately ¾ the length of the needle
  • To ensure that the needle has not inadvertently punctured a blood vessel, draw back the plunger of the syringe to see if any blood appears in the hub of the needle, if blood dose appear, simply withdraw the needle, replace it, and try again.
  • Withdraw the needle in one swift motion;
  • Gently rub the injection site to relieve discomfort.
  • Dispose of used vaccine vials and syringes in appropriate medical waste containers.

4.3.2.3.Intradermal injection (I/D)

 

Intradermal injection is a shallow or superficial injection of a substance into the dermis, which is located between the epidermis and the hypodermis.

This is very efficient immunizing route due to antigen capture and lymphatic drainage to regional lymph nodes. Smaller doses of antigen are required to achieve responses equivalent to I/M injection.

  • Preparation of vaccines should be done in a designated area away from any space where potentially contaminated items are placed.
  • Prepare vaccine when ready to administer.
  • Withdraw vaccine doses from the cool box as required.
  • Read manufacturers’ instruction of the administered route and dosage
  • Gently shake bottle before withdrawing vaccine.
  • Fix a separately placed sterile needle on automatic or manual vaccination syringe.
  • Insert a separate sterile needle in to vaccine vials and withdraw vaccine.
  • Set the dose and adjust the automatic/manual vaccination syringe according to the recommended dose to the species of animals
  • Properly restrain the animal
  • Disinfect injection site properly
  • Push the needle through the skin into the dermis,
  • Withdraw the needle in one swift motion;
  • Gently rub the injection site to relieve discomfort.
  • Dispose of used vaccine vials and syringes in appropriate medical waste containers.

 

4.3.2.4.Oral route:

 

Oral route of vaccination is administered by mouth. This route offers a convenient, powerful route for stimulating local immunity. Mass oral vaccination via drinking water has been used primarily in poultry. In oral route of vaccine administration there should be appropriate dosage calculation according to manufactures.

For example :Dissolve the 200 doses in 200 ml, the 100 doses in 100 ml and the 50 doses in 50 ml. administer by oral drench 1 ml of dissolved vaccine squirting into the beak of each bird using a clean plastic syringe.

Drinking water

The quantity of water generally required per bird for the drinking water vaccination is as follows:

For 10-14 day-old birds: 10-15 ml

For 3-8 week-old birds: 20-30 ml

For other birds:              40 ml

To calculate the volume of water required to dilute the vaccine, multiply the number of doses of the vaccine per vial by the amount of ml required per bird according to the above table.

Example, to dilute 500 doses of vaccine for 8 week-old birds, multiply 500 by 30 that means one needs 15 liters of water to dilute the 500 doses of vaccine e per vial.

Use an eyedropper. To calculate the volume of water required to dilute the vaccine into the recommended dose per vial, follow the instructions below.

Measure 1 ml of water to the dropper. Count the number of drops in this 1 ml of water. Calculate the volume of diluents required to dilute the number of doses of the vaccine per vial with the eyedropper in use.

4.4.General vaccination information

 

4.4.1.      Age at vaccination

 

  • The main obstacle to successful vaccinate young animals is the presence of blocking levels of maternally derived antibody,
  • All vaccination schemes, whether they are for an individual animal or for a herd health vaccination program must be planned considering the presence and extent of maternal antibody.

4.4.2.      Dam vaccination

 

  • Dam vaccination schemes are especially beneficial for the protection of neonates in heavily contaminated environments.
  • The strategy is to enhance neonatal immunity by augmenting colostral titers.
  • Vaccination of the dam, with a second dose just prior to parturition, maximizes colostral antibody titers. Examples of such approaches are E. coli, rota and corona virus vaccines in cows and heifers.

 

 

4.4.3.      Vaccination in infected population

 

  • The use of vaccine in endemically infected populations is strategically aimed at containment, elimination or eradication.
  • Containment requires only a moderately effective vaccine used in a highest risk individual such as the young, or animals being introduced into a new population, such as entering the breeding herd or on arrival at the feedlot.
  • High standards of management, particularly maintaining completeness of the vaccination program, hygiene and quarantining of newly introduced animals, is vital.
  • Eradication requires highly efficacious vaccines administered intensively and widely.
  • The proportions of the population, which must be vaccinated, can be estimated based on knowledge of the epidemiology of each agent but for highly contagious infections may be as high as 95%.

4.4.4.      Precautions and contraindication of vaccines

 

  • Sick animals shouldn’t be vaccinated
  • Animals under immunosuppressive drug treatment should not be vaccinated within three to four weeks
  • Care should be taken in the use of antibiotics when a vaccine containing live bacteria is administered.
  • During mass vaccination of multi-age group with live vaccines, the transmission of infection due to the organisms in the vaccine to susceptible young animals should be considered.
  • The full vaccination course as recommended by manufacturer should always be administered
  • Stressing of animals to be vaccinated should be avoided.
  • Don’t vaccinate through dirty, wet skin.
  • Repeated use of needles and syringes within herd/flock is undesirable.
  • Containers that have held live vaccines can be potentially hazardous and should be kept/disposed safe.
  • Injectable vaccines should be stored and reconstituted as recommended by the manufacturer.
  • Liquid preparations should always be adequately shaken before use to ensure uniformity of the material to be injected.
  • Accidental self-injection with oil-based vaccines can cause intense vascular spasm, which may result in loss of limbs. Therefore when happened prompt medical attention has to be taken. (STG for vet practice P. 449 --454).

5.      SOPS FOR VETERINARY TREATMENT

 

These Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) consider different routes of drug administration, treatment procedure, injection sites of different animal species, responsibility, equipment and consumable material required to enhance quality of the services provided.

5.1.General principles of the treatment

 

Most of the drugs can be administered by different routes. Drugs and patient related factors determine the selection of routes for drug administration. The factors are:

  • Characteristics of the drug.
  • Emergency/routine use.
  • Site of action of the drug—local or systemic.
  • Condition of the patient (unconscious).
  • Age of the patient.
  • Effect of gastric pH, digestive enzymes and first pass metabolism.
  • Patient’s/doctor’s choice

5.2.Drug dose calculation

 

Dose is the quantity of the drug to be administered at one time and expressed in mg/kg or IU/kg.

5.3.Preparing for drug injection

  • Always wear gloves and protective cloth to maintain the hygiene: ensure appropriate PPE is used to protect handler from accidental injury or exposure to blood and other body fluids. Personal protective equipment’s are:
  1. Gowns or coveralls 2.
  2. Steel-toed shoes or boots
  3. Disposable gloves (e.g., latex, nitrile)
  4. Eye protection
  • Restrain the animal before giving the injection.
  • Gather all the equipment’s required to administer the medication
  • Select the syringe or dosing gun
  • Choose a needle based on the animal's weight.
  • Inspect the syringe and needle. Be sure the syringe and needle are in unopened, sterile packaging that shows no evidence of damage or deterioration
  • Always read and follow the directions on the label of the medication for the dosage and route of administration
  • Verify expiry date and check for particulates, discoloration, or loss of integrity (sterility) of the drugs.

5.4.Drug administration

 

5.4.1.      Intramuscular route of administration

 

Considerations

Intramuscular injection requires an understanding of anatomical landmarks as well as precise technique. It also requires consideration of density or viscosity of the drug to be administered, size of the patient, and desired depth of injection.

Purpose

Intramuscular injections are absorbed faster than subcutaneous injections. This is because muscle tissue has a greater blood supply than the tissue just under the skin. Muscle tissue can also hold a larger volume of medication than subcutaneous tissue.

Responsibility

Veterinary doctors, Bachelor of veterinary science (BVS) and animal health assistances have the responsibility to give injection.

Materials and equipments

  • Sterile hypodermic needle,
  • Syringe
  • Drug or substance to be injected
  • Alcohol swab or alcohol moistened cotton ball

Injection site selection

  • For intramuscular injection use thick muscles away from vessels and nerves or sensitive tissues.
  • Cattle and Buffaloes - given in gluteous muscle and sometimes in neck muscles or in thigh muscles.
  • In horses and donkeys-given mostly in neck muscles,
  1. Cervical musculature - within the triangle formed by the cervical vertebra (ventrally), cranial border of the scapula (caudally), and the nuchal ligament (dorsally).
  2. Semitendinosus and semimembranosus muscles- the uppermost landmark is the tuber ischia and the distal landmark is at the location where the muscle begins to merge with the tendons.
  3. Pectoral muscles- in centre of muscle between front limbs

       

  • In Sheep, Goats, Dog and Cats- given mostly in the medial aspect of thigh muscles
  1. Quadriceps IM injections- the quadriceps muscle group is located cranial to the greater trochanter of femur, ventral to the wing of the ilium and proximal to the straight patellar ligament of the stifle.
  2. Triceps IM Injections- the triceps muscle is located caudal to point of shoulder, ventral to the scapula, continuing distally to the elbow.
  3. Lumbar IM Injections- the epaxial muscles are located dorsal to the line of transverse processes of the vertebrae and lateral to the dorsal spinous processes.
  4. Hamstring IM Injections - the hamstring muscle group (semimembranosus,
  • In chicken – given in Chest or pectoral muscle.
  • In Pig-given in thigh muscle for intramuscular injection

Procedure

  • Assemble the syringe and needle, shake the bottle, and swab the cap with clean surgical spirit.
  • Always check the recommended dose on the bottle label.
  • Sometimes air bubbles are visible in the syringe. Tap the syringe gently while the needle is still in the medication vial.
  • Thrust the needle through the rubber cap of the bottle.
  • Turn the bottle upside down and push the plunger to inject the air in the syringe into the bottle
  • Make sure that the needle tip is below the surface of the fluid.
  • Pull the plunger down, drawing slightly more liquid into the syringe than required.
  • Push the plunger slightly to expel any air bubbles and adjust the right dose.
  • Detach the syringe, leaving the needle in the cap for next dose withdrawal.
  • Finally, attach a second needle to the syringe; expel any air from the needle.
  • Identify, clean, disinfect and dry the injection site using disinfectant (savlon, alcohol) before and after injection by cotton.
  • Insert the needle quickly straight into the muscle, at a 90-degree orientation up to the hub drug administration in respectively.
  • Before injecting, draw the plunger out slightly to check if the needle has entered a blood vessel. If blood enters the syringe, withdraw the needle slightly and redirect into the muscle.
  • Inject the drug properly and gently at appropriate site of injection.

Potential Adverse Effects

  • Trauma
  • Bruising, lacerations
  • Hematoma formation
  • Spread of disease- Injection with multiple use needles can spread anaplasmosis, bovine leucosis and other diseases.

 

5.4.2.      Intravenous route of drug administration

 

      Considerations

  • Advantages
  • Bioavailability is 100%.
  • Quick onset of action; therefore, it is the route of choice in emergency
  • Large volume of fluid can be administered, e.g. intravenous fluids in patients with severe dehydration.
  • Highly irritant drugs can be given because they get diluted in blood.
  • Hypertonic solution can be infused by intravenous route, e.g. 20% mannitol in cerebral oedema.
  • Disadvantages
  • Once the drug is injected, its action cannot be halted.
  • Local irritation may cause phlebitis.
  • Strict aseptic conditions are needed.
  • Extravasation of some drugs can cause injury, necrosis and sloughing of tissues.
  • Precautions
  • Drug should usually be injected slowly.
  • Before injecting, make sure that the tip of the needle is in the vein.
  • Syringe must be free from air bubble

 Purpose

    To describes procedure of intravenous injection. Drugs are injected directly into the blood stream through a vein. Intravenous injection gives the response is very rapid, the dosage of the drug can be easily controlled, and veins are insensitive to irritation by irritant drugs at higher concentration.

Responsibility-Veterinary doctors, Bachelor of veterinary science (BVS) and animal health assistances are responsible for injection.

Materials and Equipment’s

  • Restraint (e.g., halter, squeeze chute)
  • Sterile gloves
  • Needles and syringes
  • Gauze
  • Drugs

Site of Injection

  • In cattle, buffaloes, horses, sheep, goats - IV injection given in Jugular vein
  • In dogs and cats - IV injection given in saphenous vein and Cephalic vein
  • In Chicken - use wing vein for Iv injection
  • In Pig - use Ear vein for IV injection

         Note: - In female ruminants sometimes we can use Milk vein for IV injection

Procedure

  • Assemble the syringe and needle, shake the bottle, and swab the cap with clean surgical spirit.
  • Verify expiry date and check for particulates, discoloration, or loss of integrity (sterility) of the drugs.
  • Always check the recommended dose on the bottle label.
  • Inspect the syringe and needle. Be sure the syringe and needle are in unopened, sterile packaging that shows no evidence of damage or deterioration
  • Sometimes air bubbles are visible in the syringe. Tap the syringe gently while the needle is still in the medication vial.
  • Thrust the needle through the rubber cap of the bottle.
  • Turn the bottle upside down and push the plunger to inject the air in the syringe into the bottle
  • Make sure that the needle tip is below the surface of the fluid.
  • Pull the plunger down, drawing slightly more liquid into the syringe than required.
  • Push the plunger slightly to expel any air bubbles and adjust the right dose.
  • Detach the syringe, leaving the needle in the cap for next dose withdrawal.
  • Finally, attach a second needle to the syringe; expel any air from the needle.
  • Identify, clean, disinfect and dry the injection site using disinfectant (savlon, alcohol) before and after injection by cotton.
  • Clip area over the vein. Wipe with antiseptic gauze to remove superficial dirt and debris.
  • Occlude vein by applying pressure at the base of the groove and visualize raised vein.
  • Introduce needle into the vein.
  • A flash of blood into the needle hub confirms correct placement then inject the drug.
  • Inject the drug slowly and gently at appropriate site of injection.

 Potential Adverse Effects

  • Hematoma- apply pressure
  • Thrombosis/Thromboembolism

Contact a qualified veterinarian for treatment recommendations if any of the following are noted.

  • Heat, pain, swelling first noted at the injection site, purulent material draining from the injection site.
  • Induration (hardening) of the vessel
  • Stiff, swelling of injection site
  • Pyrexia, local or systemic infections, septic shock
  • Thromboembolism may occur secondarily to thrombosis

5.4.3.      Subcutaneous route of drug administration

 

Considerations

During subcutaneous injection, the bleb of fluids should be gently compressed and spread out by the clinician to distribute the fluids, improve absorption, and decrease leakage following withdrawal of the needle. Subcutaneous injections of irritating drugs or dextrose containing solutions must be avoided.

 

Purpose

To describes procedures for subcutaneous injection. These route of injection is Preferred when slow and continuous absorption of drug is required. The injected drug disperses through the loose connective tissues. They dissolve in tissue fluid before it can enter either capillaries or lymphatic tissue.

ﬂuid before it can enter either capillaries or lymphatic

Responsibility

Veterinary doctors, Bachelor of veterinary science (BVS) and animal health assistances are responsible to give the injection.

 

Materials and Equipments

  • Proper restraint
  • Needles (16-20- gauge 1-1.5 inch)
  • Syringe
  • Small square of gauze (alcohol soaked)
  • Drug

Site of Injection

  • In Cattle - subcutaneous injection is given caudal to the forelimb at the level of the mid-thorax and cranial to the forelimb in the caudal cervical region where loose skin can be grasped easily
  • In Dog and Cat - most often dorsal cervical region, dorsal inter scapular area and lateral surfaces of the thorax is used
  • In Equine - any skin site may be used but preferred areas are the neck

 

Procedure

  • Assemble the syringe and needle, shake the bottle, and swab the cap with clean surgical spirit.
  • Verify expiry date and check for particulates, discoloration, or loss of integrity (sterility) of the drugs.
  • Always check the recommended dose on the bottle label.
  • Inspect the syringe and needle. Be sure the syringe and needle are in unopened, sterile packaging that shows no evidence of damage or deterioration
  • Sometimes air bubbles are visible in the syringe. Tap the syringe gently while the needle is still in the medication vial.
  • Thrust the needle through the rubber cap of the bottle.
  • Turn the bottle upside down and push the plunger to inject the air in the syringe into the bottle
  • Make sure that the needle tip is below the surface of the fluid.
  • Pull the plunger down, drawing slightly more liquid into the syringe than required.
  • Push the plunger slightly to expel any air bubbles and adjust the right dose.
  • Detach the syringe, leaving the needle in the cap for next dose withdrawal.
  • Finally, attach a second needle to the syringe; expel any air from the needle.
  • Identify, clean, disinfect and dry the injection site using disinfectant (savlon, alcohol) before and after injection by cotton.
  • Small needles (5/8th inch 25 gauge) are typically used.
  • Quickly stick the needle through the skin at low angle approximately ¼-1/2 inch.
  • Attach the drug-filled syringe to the needle and aspirate to ensure that inadvertent venipuncture did not occur. If blood is present at aspiration, redirect the needle and aspirate again.
  • Place a finger over the skin where the drug will be deposited. Inject drug.
  • The injection should be easy – if not, redirect the needle slightly deeper.
  • The finger should feel a ‘bleb’ under the skin.

Potential Adverse Effects

  • Abscess formation
  • The skin should be cleaned thoroughly prior to placing a needle
  • If an abscess forms, contact veterinary staff.
  • Muscle soreness
  • Avoid repeated injections at the same site to minimize this complication
  • If soreness affects the horse’s ability to normally function (graze, walk comfortably), contact veterinary staff.
  • Swelling
  • If accompanied by tenderness and/or stiffness, contact veterinary staff.

Post drug injection

  • Withdraw the needle in one swift motion
  • Gently rub the injection site to relieve discomfort.
  • Properly dispose all used treatment vials, syringes and needles in a puncture proof container
  • Seal empty and partly used medicine bottles in separate transparent plastic bags and mark properly.
  • Maintain carful records to identify each and every treated animal.
  • Needles should be changed or sterilized after injection.
  • Always give an advice and consult for the client about the given drugs of the withdrawal time, side effect, frequency of the treatment and management of the treated animal.
  • Always discard partly-used bottles of medicine, disposable syringe and other disposable material at the end of the day.

5.4.4.      Oral drug administration

 

Oral dosage forms refer to administration of drug through the mouth. The most commonly used preparations are solid oral dosage forms such as tablets, capsules, granules, powder, paste and boluses

  1. Drug administration using balling gun

Considerations

Always ensure that the handler is competent and that the person administering the drug has the equipment and drug ready to ensure that the procedure takes as short a time as possible for the animal.

  • The head will need to be raised until the animal swallows to ensure that the drug is ingested.
  • Ensure there is no food in the mouth before starting the administration technique.
  • Ensure the full dose is consumed; if any amount of drug is lost or dropped from the mouth, re-dosing will be necessary.

Purpose

To describes procedures to administrator of the bolus using a balling gun. Many drugs are designed for use in tablet, bolus or powder form, given directly into the mouth or mixed in food

Materials required

  • Balling gun
  • Drug(Bolus)

Procedures    

  • Restrain the animal
  • Measure the body weight of the animal by using the different measuring instruments (e.g heart girth rate, weight balance…)
  • Calculate appropriate  dosage according to manufactures
  • Prepare and Clean the balling gun
  • Place the animal in a restraining or squeeze chute with a head gate
  • Restring animal with nose tongs and a rope in its mouth
  • Use the balling gun to administer boluses
  • Insert bolus into the opening of the balling gun
  • And then, pushed out by the plunger and Put the bolus on the base of the tongue.
  • Check the bolus is placed behind the tongue
  • Properly withdraw the balling gun from the animal after administrated the drug
  • Check and Follow up the animal is taken the drug properly
  • Always give an advice and consult for the client about the given drugs of the withdrawal time, side effect, frequency of the treatment and management of the  treated animal.

 2. Drenching and Tubing drug administration

Considerations

Never pour liquids directly into an animal’s nose: this will result in severe inflammation of the nasal cavity and pharynx and most likely result in aspiration pneumonia.

Purpose

To describes procedures to administrator drug by drenching and Tubing. Drenching is the technique of administration of liquid products directly into the back of the mouth and down the throat.

Materials Required

  • Stomach tube rounded at the end and smooth, so as not to damage the delicate nasal mucosa. Soaking in hot water before use will make the tube more flexible.
  • A large funnel which should fit the other end of the tube tightly.
  • Drug

Procedure

  • Restrain the animal
  • Measure the body weight of the animal by using the different measuring instruments (e.g heart girth rate, weight balance…)
  • calculate appropriate  dosage according to manufactures
  • prepare the required materials
  • Checks the tube is soft and blunt the edge on the tube with appropriate an inner diameter depending on the size of the animal.
  • Estimate the length and mark on the tube before it is inserted into the animal’s mouth.
  • Lubricated the end of the tube using edible oil, and then placed the tube in the animal’s mouth and slowly pushed towards the stomach.
  • For equines insert the tube through the left side of nostril and slowly pushed towards the stomach
  • Check the position to make sure the tube has not entered the trachea and travelled to the lungs.
  • Check the location of the tube in the stomach is indicated by possible smell of rumen gases.
  • Apply the prescribed drug slowly.
  • After administrated the drug withdraw the nasal tube/stomach tube from animal gently
  • Wash and clean the tube

 3.  Drug administration with feed and water

Purpose

To describes procedures to administrator drug with feed and water. This technique of administration forms a paste by mixing the powder or tablets with water and feed.

Considerations

Appropriate dosage calculation according to manufactures

 

Responsibility

Veterinary doctors, Bachelor of veterinary science (BVS) and animal health assistances are responsible to give the injection.

Materials and Equipments

  • Palatable feed/clean water
  • Drug
  • Dish / Materials for mixing of feed /water and drug

Procedure

Two techniques are as follows:

 

  1. Roll a bolus by mixing tablets or powder with thick molasses, jaggery (unrefined cane sugar) or other locally available palatable substances. Offer the mixture to the animal to eat and supervise until finished.
  2. Form a paste by mixing the powder with water. Draw this mixture up into a plastic syringe. Raise the head, open the mouth and deposit the drug on the back of the tongue or in the cheek pouch. If a smaller amount is to be administered it might be possible to deposit this under the tongue. Close the mouth and keep the head raised until the paste has been swallowed.
  • Prepare palatable feed/clean water
  • calculate appropriate  dosage according to manufactures
  • Appropriately mix /dissolve the drug with feed/ water respectively
  • Before giving the mixed/dissolved drug ,the animal must be fasting or thirsting
  • Give the mixed/dissolved drug for the animal
  • Check the administrated drug whether the animal taken properly

5.4.5.      Topical drug application

 

It refers to external application of drug to the body surface for localized action at accessible site such as skin, eyes, body oriﬁces and body cavity.

 

  1. Drug application as dropping (Ointment) form
  • Restrain the animal properly
  • Wash your hands before and after administering medication thoroughly with soap and water
  • Avoid touching the tip of the tube against the animal eye or anything else - the medication and its container must be kept clean.
  • Holding the tube between your thumb and forefinger, place it as near to animal eyelid as possible without touching it.
  • Brace the remaining fingers of that hand against the animal head.
  • Slowly squeeze ribbon of ointment or dropintothe inside edge of the lower eye lidto dispense the liquid medication.
  • Consult and advice the client to be follow up the prescribed medicine.

    2. Creamy medication

  • Always wear gloves and protective cloth to administer
  • Prepare the required equipment’s
  • Prepare the prescribed drug
  • Restrain the animal properly
  • Wash, clean and dry the affected part the animal body
  • Shave the periphery affected part of the skin
  • Apply the prescribed drug gently and protect the treated part of the skin
  • Consult and advice the client about the management of animal

5.4.6.      Spray and Dipping for Ecto-parasite

 

  1. Spraying
  • Always Wear protective clothing ,goggles and face mask to avoid any chemical contact 
  • Prepare the required equipment’s and drug
  • Measure  and dilute appropriate drug  according to the manufactures
  • Restrain the animal properly
  • Spray the sides and flanks in a zigzag pattern.
  • Spray along the entire length of the back, the brisket, each leg , belly, udder or scrotum, tail ,anal area.
  • Finally, spray the head, face, neck and ears.
  • Wash your hand  with soap and water after medication

     2.Dipping

  • Always wear gloves and protective cloth
  • Clean and dry the dirty animals before dipping
  • Restrain the animal properly
  • Measure and dilate the accarcide
  • Dipping the animal properly 
  • After dipping, kept the animal in the sun to dry.

 Precautions for dipping and spraying

  • Don’t spray animal in a confined, non-ventilated area.
  • Don`t dips animal when they are thirsty or overheated.
  • Don`t contaminates feed or drinking water.
  • Don`t apply insecticides to sick animals or animals under stress.
  • Don`t  apply insecticides to lambs less than 3 months old, and use light applications on lambs 3 to 6 months old.
  • Don`t  treat animal just before slaughtering
  • Don`t  eat ,smoke or drink while handling chemicals
  • Store all acarcides in their original container away from food or feed and out of reach of children and pets.
  • Do not pour into rivers or ponds any unused solution dip contents
